My Recommendations

Below are reviews for some of my favorite roguelikes that I highly recommend. I opted to highlight just 8 games to keep things concise. Each game offers its own unique elements, so whether you're familiar with roguelikes or just starting out, there should be something here for you. My scores reflect my personal experience, but I encourage you to explore and see what resonates with you.

Before diving into the reviews, let's clarify a few nuances:

Roguelike vs. Roguelite

There is a difference here, mainly when it comes to meta progression. Roguelikes typically have permanent death and no global upgrades that affect your future runs. Roguelites, on the other hand, also have permanent death but let you earn currency/unlockables that grants global upgrades, making each run a bit easier as you go. Most of the games I'll be discussing are considered roguelites, but for consistency—and because the terms get mixed up a lot—I'll just refer to everything as roguelikes.

I'll be honest: my experience with classic roguelikes is pretty limited. They often play like old-school 'pen and paper RPGs', which makes them a bit tough for me to get into. But don't worry—I've got a review of a classic roguelike at the bottom of the page that I think is worth checking out!

Ascension System

This term may come up frequently in my reviews. I borrowed the concept from Slay the Spire, which was the first roguelike to bring this mechanic to mainstream attention. An ascension system typically involves progressively increasing the difficulty of the game with each run, often introducing new challenges and rewards as players improve their skills and strategies. This innovative approach inspired many other developers to incorporate similar mechanics into their own games.

Now that we've got that out of the way, here are some of my recommendations:

A strong contender for anyone's top 3, Slay the Spire is one of the most acclaimed roguelikes—and indie games—out there, and for good reason. In this deckbuilder, you ascend a spire, battling enemies and building your deck along the way. It's one of my all-time favorites, nearly perfect in my view. It's also the kind of game that even non-gamers could easily get hooked on.
Grade
Replayability - 5/5
This is a "desert island" game, with mechanics and nuances that can still surprise you after hundreds of hours.
Difficulty Curve - 4/5
While RNG plays a role, solid game knowledge can make winning at lower difficulties nearly routine. True challenge lies in Ascension 20 runs, where consistent wins are reserved for top players.
Meta Progression - 2/5
Progression includes unlocking cards, relics, and characters, which happens fairly quickly. The main goal shifts to conquering all ascension levels with each character.
Complexity - 5/5
Early game choices can heavily impact the final act. To win consistently, you must be meticulous with your strategy.

Another classic, The Binding of Isaac has undergone extensive development. You play as Isaac (and others), navigating Zelda-inspired dungeons. The game boasts incredible depth, featuring over 700 items in the vanilla version, along with numerous unlockables, bosses, and endings. On average, it takes about 500 hours to 100% complete the game.
Grade
Replayability - 5/5
This game is perfect for endless play. With a vast array of items, the potential for unique synergies is staggering, estimated at ±531^216.
Difficulty Curve - 4/5
Similar to Slay the Spire, RNG significantly influences runs, and success heavily depends on strategic decision-making.
Meta Progression - 3/5
While there's no formal meta progression, the focus is on unlocking new characters and defeating every boss with each character, which provides a substantial challenge.
Complexity - 4/5
Basic game knowledge can take you far, but the numerous item synergies allow for the creation of incredibly powerful builds.

From the creators of FTL, Into the Breach is an engaging mech strategy game. You control three time-traveling mech pilots as you battle to prevent an alien invasion. The combat resembles chess, with each mech possessing unique attacks and abilities within a grid-based layout. Although it doesn't align with my personal interests (I'm not a fan of mech themes), the gameplay loop is highly enjoyable and offers a depth comparable to FTL.
Grade
Replayability - 4/5
Similar to FTL, you can unlock various pilots through challenges, allowing for optimal crew combinations.
Difficulty Curve - 4/5
This game requires similar brainpower to FTL. Understanding your pilots' strengths and weaknesses and building an effective team comp demands considerable game knowledge.
Meta Progression - 1/5
Like FTL, progression only unlocks more mech pilots without any global changes.
Complexity - 3/5
The combat feels akin to chess. Mastering the mechanics and understanding the traits of enemies and pilots requires you to plan your moves 3-5 steps ahead.

One of the few cooking roguelikes, Plate Up! offers a fun, casual experience, especially with friends. You run a restaurant, preparing meals and serving customers, using your earnings to upgrade the establishment each day. If you've played Overcooked, you'll feel right at home with the gameplay.
Grade
Replayability - 3/5
The game shines with friends, otherwise I feel that there is a lack of variety in items and events.
Difficulty Curve - 4/5
Casual players can easily beat the game, but the real challenge lies in the endless levels after "beating" your restaurant. Mastering automation to streamline your restaurant is particularly difficult.
Meta Progression - 1/5
Similar to Slay the Spire, there are only a few unlockables that you can obtain quickly.
Complexity - 2/5
Aside from the automation meta, the game remains quite casual.
Classic Roguelike Showcase
Game
Review
Caves of Qud is one of the most notable titles in the classic roguelike genre. It strikes a good balance between traditional mechanics and a modern interface. The game features a richly detailed world filled with deep lore and unique characters, allowing players to immerse themselves in its intricate narratives.

One of its standout elements is the character creation system, where you can choose from a variety of mutations—like growing extra limbs or harnessing the power of the elements—each with its own gameplay implications. Each run offers a vast array of choices, from how you approach exploration to the tactical decision-making required in combat against the strange NPCs you encounter. Aside from the questline and the towns, everything is procedurally generated, offering a fresh experience every time you dive into the depths, whether you're exploring ancient ruins or engaging in thrilling encounters in the wild.

I personally consider it the most beginner-friendly classic roguelike for those looking to explore the genre. It's the only one I've tried that truly hooked me—so much so that I had to force myself to stop, while I wait for the 1.0 release this winter after a 15+ year-long development cycle. I'm eagerly anticipating that before I dive deeper into the game.
